4. (some facts about the mitochondrial genome's) divergence across organisms:
    The mitochondrial genome varies widely among species in size, gene content, and structure:
        1. Animals: Compact genomes with conserved gene order and minimal non-coding regions.
        2. Plants: Large, highly variable mitochondrial genomes with frequent introns, repeats, and horizontal gene transfer.
        3. Fungi and Protists: Extremely diverse genomes; some have fragmented or linear mtDNA.
    Despite this variation, core genes for respiration and translation are conserved.

5. Presence of the Mitochondrial Genome in Various Organisms
    1. Animals: Animal mitochondrial genomes are small (~16 kb in humans), encoding 37 genes: 13 protein-coding genes, 22 tRNAs, and 2 rRNAs.
    2. Plants: Plant mitochondrial genomes are significantly larger and more complex. They can include 40â€“60 genes but may reach sizes of hundreds of kilobases due to repeats, introns, and foreign DNA.
    3. Fungi: Fungal mitochondrial genomes vary, with some maintaining highly reduced genomes, while others show unique structural variations, such as introns and plasmid-like elements.
    4. Protists: Protist mitochondria exhibit extreme diversity. For instance, Plasmodium falciparum (malaria parasite) has a highly reduced mitochondrial genome (~6 kb), while others are more complex.
    5. Metazoans vs. Non-Metazoans: Metazoan mitochondrial genomes are typically more streamlined, whereas non-metazoan genomes (e.g., plants, fungi) often retain remnants of their bacterial ancestry.

6. Fundamental facts about the mt genome:
    - The mitochondrial genome is typically circular and double-stranded, though exceptions (e.g., linear forms) exist in some species.
    - Its size is small compared to nuclear genomes, ranging from about 15-20 kilobases in animals (e.g., humans) to hundreds of kilobases in some plants and protists.
    - It is compact, containing relatively few non-coding regions and high gene density.
